Profile | Tania Nanevicz

Cardiologist Tania Nanevicz grew up in Palo Alto. She never intended to grow old here: but then she met her future husband Evan when they were medical students at UCSF. When he made a move down to Palo Alto to set up his first practice, Tania was drawn to her hometown for a host of new reasons. Ultimately, she and Evan married and moved into his Eichler near Ross Road. Her sister moved in just a few doors down, and thus began a new family tradition: sending the next generation of cousins to the same school!

“When I first heard about Partners in Education, my nine-year-old was in kindergarten. I knew how important support for teachers is (both my mother and sister are teachers), but everyone was still trying to understand how PiE differed from the traditional giving channels through the PTA,” explains Tania.

Once Tania understood that PiE offered an efficient way for all children in the PAUSD to get increased funding for science, math and art enrichment, plus more valuable aide time in the classroom, she was moved to volunteer to help run the PiE campaign at her children’s elementary school. When the job went from co-chair to chair, she was leery about taking on such a workload given her other responsibilities, which is when her sister stepped in and offered to co-chair. Just another family-centered approach to getting the job done. “Nicola and I work well together. I have loved getting to know volunteers from all over the district who share my passion for education. We know that by supporting education, we are taking care of the entire community.”

There are so many ways that PiE has added to the quality of life for the Nanevicz clan. Tania and Evan met playing in an orchestra, and they both keep up with their instruments. They both believe that the arts promote human development in a holistic way. Having access to the arts in public school classrooms is the best way they know to reinforce the values they instill at home. Furthermore, aides in the classroom not only meet expanding class sizes head on, but they also relieve teachers of a certain amount of busy work. This lends itself to teacher retention, for good teachers need to be freed up to do what they do best: imparting information in new and creative ways.

When the discussion turns to public education in the state of California, Tania says, “This country was built on equal access to education; we should know better than to chip away at this fundamental right. Our children are our most important assets. I think it is absolutely devastating what is going on in terms of budget cuts to education in California, and although Palo Alto has been shielded from much of the shock, we have to be prepared for the worst. PiE is perfectly positioned to meet the challenges we face.”
