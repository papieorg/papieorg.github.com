Donor Profile: We Give!


Profile | Lisa Young Hallenbeck

Lisa Young Hallenbeck started supporting PiE as a kinder parent. “Our school tells us from day one: PTA provides ‘stuff’ for our classrooms, and PiE pays for ‘staff.’ After volunteering at school, I see the value of both. PiE provides such great support in classrooms. It’s a well-run organization and I love the fact that funds are distributed across the entire district. PiE is communal, supporting all schools, and I see a lot of value in contributing to the greater good of the school community.”

Volunteering with the Spectra Art program, funded by PiE, has become a passion for Lisa. “I love art and I really wanted to volunteer in it. It’s been great to see how Spectra art works in the classroom. Teachers tie art to the curriculum, so it adds a different perspective on the material. Kids who may not be the most confident in science or math can be really confident in art. What they create is their own. With art, they build confidence to take on other subjects.”

Lisa is convinced that Spectra Art teaches vital lessons to children. “Sometimes, kids are labeled—by other kids, parents, teachers—but in art, the labels don’t translate. Kids who may not be able to sit still bring an ability to listen to art. They have enthusiasm, they try new things, they take a risk—all things they might not otherwise do.”

Lisa believes that art also helps kids build their creativity of thought. “As kids progress, for many, their thinking may grow more linear and rigid. Through art we try to teach them that things don’t have to be perfect, and that a mistake can actually point them in the direction of something else, something better. Art can be beautiful even if it’s nothing like the teacher’s example. And though the teacher might do a project by doing A, then B, then C, it’s all right if a student approaches it by doing C then B then C again and then A. There is no one right way to do art.”

Art conveys other important lessons. “With art, we can take materials that are found—a plastic bottle, a cork, moss, leaves—and make art. It’s a great lesson in a throwaway society, that they can make something amazing from materials that would otherwise be trash or recycling.”  Art can also help with more practical skills. Lisa points out, “Art can be very helpful in developing fine motor skills. Careful detailing in art can help kids who may struggle with their writing. Kids benefit from the process of practicing art.”

Art lessons also provide a very enjoyable part of the day for students.  Lisa observes, “Often we focus on science and math as a district and I worry that some adults don’t value what art has to offer. But I’ve seen it every time I volunteer. Kids may be bored, not excited at all, but then they get the chance to work with their hands and they get excited, enthusiastic, invested. And some kids become experts on different art medium, like water color pencils or pastels. They can’t wait to show me what they can do! They have so much enthusiasm, they really blossom. And that is thanks to PiE funding art.”

Lisa believes that PiE is crucial for Palo Alto schools. “PiE fills a definite need in the community. It’s a good organization with a lot of integrity and a great reputation. PiE is transparent and parents have some say in how the funds are spent. And, contributing to PiE is a lot cheaper than private school tuition! I hope everyone will support PiE and the great programs it brings to our schools, including Spectra Art.”

Lisa and her husband both grew up in Palo Alto, attending Van Auken/Los Ninos and Green Gables, Jordan, and Paly. Lisa then deserted her Palo Alto roots to become a lifelong Cal fanatic while her husband went on to Middlebury. Today Lisa is retired from Odwalla and her husband works in high tech product management.
