Profile | Mike Dreyfus

In 2000, realtor Mike Dreyfus walked away from an enviable career as a top five broker for Coldwell Banker in southern California and relocated his wife and three young daughters from Newport Beach to Palo Alto to follow his wife’s dream job in corporate law in Silicon Valley. 

 “We did a lot of research and discovered that the public schools in Palo Alto are amazing,” he said. “There is so much outstanding diversity between all the elementary schools, each of which has its own personality and culture, with lots of choice options. They are almost like private schools.”

When he enrolled his oldest daughter at Addison School he learned of a math information night for 5th grade. “I decided to go and was shocked at all the dads in attendance,” he related.  “That’s something you don’t see in southern California. But, I was even more shocked when the parents starting demanding more rigorous math education from the principal. I came home to my wife and said, ‘You know how we have Little League dads in Newport Beach? Well, here they have math dads!”

Dreyfus fearlessly started over when he opened Dreyfus Properties in Palo Alto as an independent realtor in the middle of the 2001 Silicon Valley bust.  It was an audacious move but one that typifies his assiduous work ethic and confidence in making his own luck.  

“I knew what I was doing but I didn’t know anyone.  I loved Palo Alto right away because it had a very similar demographic to Newport Beach, Corona del Mar and Manhattan Beach, where I had offices and sold a lot of homes,” he said.

A graduate of both UCLA and UC Hastings law school, Dreyfus had quickly learned that practicing law in Newport Beach was not the career he had hoped for. Dreyfus found his calling when his sister needed help selling her house. “I loved connecting with people to achieve their dreams, but realized that clients are not well-served by the cut-throat competitive culture of big real estate firms that emphasize profits over information-sharing.”

“Silicon Valley is all about innovation and entrepreneurial spirit and I saw a chance to put together a totally new kind of real estate firm in Palo Alto,” he said, one featuring collaboration, teamwork, information-pooling and networking between brokers so that clients obtain as much information as possible to make a good decision.

When the real estate meltdown of 2008 hit the Bay Area hard, he seized the opportunity to expand his business. “It’s contrarian thinking to expand in a down market but I knew that meant lots of movement and opportunity for properties to be sold,” he said. Dreyfus now has 20 employees, including 15 agents and hopes one day to grow to 30 or 40. In 2010 he opened a second office in Menlo Park, where he hired agents who are experts in neighborhoods across that city.

Dreyfus said he became a Palo Alto Business Partner in Education at the Summa Cum Laude level because the importance of education foundations in Palo Alto and Menlo Park cannot be overstated. “Organizations like Palo Alto’s Partners in Education allow the local schools to maintain a ‘gold standard’ in public education, and have led to increases in local property values despite the worst real estate market in U.S. history. The reason is that most parents’ first choice, no matter how rich they are, is always to send their kids to great public schools over private schools and Palo Alto is one of the few communities where that actually happens.”
