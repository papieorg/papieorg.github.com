IN OUR MIDDLE SCHOOLS

Middle School and Brain Development

The middle school brain is in transition, primed for the challenge of higher-order thinking.  This is dramatically illustrated in the development, and eventual mastery, of written communication.  PiE dollars support students’ literacy efforts by funding creative writing electives at both Terman and JLS, in addition to an innovative afterschool writing program at Jordan.

A middle student’s writing capability reflects the increased functionality of a maturing brain. Informational summaries learned in elementary school, according to Kelly Zalatimo, Instructional Supervisor in English at Jordan, are suddenly overshadowed by “analysis, abstraction, persuasion, and synthesis.” These higher-order thinking processes require more than basic training in reason and logic. They require neurological milestones that a young brain will not rush. “We see a great deal of variation among our students when it comes to abstract thinking,” points out Kari Nygaard, Instructional Supervisor in English at JLS.

At Terman, Instructional Supervisor Tracey Berliner feels an enormous responsibility for the “safe passage” of these young minds from concrete to abstract thinkers.  “We provide a lot of scaffolding in support of young writers,” Berliner says.  “We want them to know about all the tools available to them to write effectively.”  Transition words take on a whole new meaning in middle school, both as a means of bridging sentences, and also as a way of bridging concrete and abstract ideas.

At this transitional time in human brain development, each child is uniquely positioned to approach the task of writing.  Since habit-forming is a neurological process, good habits for good writing are best established from the outset.  If you think of the middle school brain as a fallow field, and higher-order thinking as the plow, the neural pathways that criss-cross these fields become a student’s habits.

Given the broad range of readiness, each student may require a unique approach to developing strong writing habits.  But teachers with four or five classes of twenty-five kids each simply cannot meet the demand for personal response. Thanks in part to seed funding provided by PiE, Jordan has a new after-school writing and tutoring program that draws from all disciplines as faculty tutors work one-on-one with students in fifteen-minute coaching sessions.  “Getting extra help with my writing reassures me that I am on the right track,” explains a Jordan sixth grader. A similar program at Terman supports students who struggle with higher-order writing skills.

At JLS, Nygaard became aware of a pervasive interest among students in creative writing.  For several years, she supervised the publication of a literary magazine as part of a student club. The popularity of the club increased and Nygaard ultimately petitioned for formal inclusion of creative writing in the JLS curriculum, as at Terman.  Now in its second year, the creative writing elective has thrived.  Enthuses one Terman seventh-grader, “Creative writing is a safe place to share my work with people who, like me, are inspired by words and ideas.”  Adds her teacher, “I can see a positive impact on her expository writing in English class as well."
