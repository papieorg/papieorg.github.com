IN OUR ELEMENTARY SCHOOLS

New Leadership, New Learning

“We are living in amazing times of change as a society and as a nation,” says Addison principal Jocelyn Garcia-Thome, whose words ring true for the Palo Alto Unified School District as well. The changeover in leadership throughout the district is at an historic high. Since Superintendent Kevin Skelly was hired four years ago, retirements and promotions have brought new leadership to 16 of the 17 PAUSD schools. Fortunately for PAUSD, many of the new leaders have a history within the district. “We’re getting a good mix of bringing folks in from the outside with other experiences to share, in addition to people who know and have an understanding of the particular schools in which they are working,” says Skelly. “I like the new talent that we have. I think they’re bringing new ideas, they’re honoring the work that we’re doing, and they’ve got lots of energy for the work they want to see happen.”

Energy is evident in the work Garcia-Thome is doing at Addison. “How is the information age going to evolve?” Garcia-Thome, now in her second year as Principal, asks. It’s a question she considers carefully after working for the past 10 years at HP and IBM, where she came to appreciate how important the efficient use of both technology and team collaboration is to success in any venture. Combining her business experience with her previous 10 years serving as Principal in the Campbell Union School District has guided her to focus on skills students will need in the future: the ability to provide adaptive leadership, strong project management skills, and a solid ability to solve tough problems. In addition, the ability to collaborate and an increased cultural awareness will be required skills for success in any field.

Danae Reynolds, Principal at Escondido, couldn’t agree more. “As children are exposed to, and develop friendships and relationships with people from around the world, they develop shared missions and values,” Reynolds says. “As they do, the world becomes smaller, and children are able to interact and learn from each other.” Although this is Reynolds’ first year as Principal, she has been both an individual school and district-level Reading Specialist for the past 11 years. Reynolds appreciates that PiE funds provide instructional aides in most PAUSD elementary classrooms, which she believes is critical to the small group, and one-one instruction, young readers require while developing fundamental reading skills.

“Reading is critical,” according to Mary Pat O’Connell, Nixon’s principal for the past four years. “It is the foundation for learning everything else.” O’Connell focuses her efforts on preparing all students for the new economy.  “The evolving nature of work requires a high level of education,” she adds. “To prepare all of our children for this workplace has to be our mission.”  And that mission is supported with PiE dollars. “PiE provides us with the opportunity to focus on things that matter,” she says. And it’s not just the ability to read that is emphasized. Providing guided reading practice, referring students to specialists when appropriate, supporting older elementary school students with how to concisely summarize reading material and effectively communicate what they’ve learned are all important skills supported by Instructional Aides and Reading Specialists.” Supporting teachers, according to O’Connell, is the most important work we do as a community. “The research is clear. What makes the biggest difference in a high quality educational learning environment is the teacher.”
