IN OUR ELEMENTARY SCHOOLS

Enrichment Boosts Brain Power

The creative brain is the new currency, according to Daniel Goleman, author of Emotional Intelligence.  The notion that creativity is only for artsy types is outdated, as recent brain research shows the creative brain state accesses a whole range of neural connections throughout the brain.

This is abundantly clear if you visit any of our PAUSD elementary schools, where PiE dollars support 100% of the Spectra Art program, in addition to innovative movement courses and hands-on science.  “The creative process,” according to Shelley Carson, author of Your Creative Brain, “is less about talent and more of a broad-based style of thinking that we all can learn.”

At Walter Hays, Dr. Jane Benson’s Perpetual Motor program, funded with PiE dollars, includes a unique opportunity to access classroom curriculum through movement.  This kinesthetic approach enhances the learning experience by adding physical expression to intellectual study.  For younger students, Benson may instruct children to form the shapes of the alphabet with their bodies as they learn to read.  With older students, Benson may incorporate historic children’s games into a classroom study of a particular period in history.  Since Benson is able to work with children over time, from kindergarten through fifth grade, she can tailor activities to specifically meet students’ growing needs.

The creative process is also embraced daily in the PiE-funded Spectra Arts program at district elementary schools.  For students in Julie Bagniefski and Avery Olesen’s second-third-grade classroom at Ohlone, what started out as a Spectra Arts project last spring, turned into a creative world journey and the student-led development of an entrepreneurial business.  Working with an ongoing classroom social studies theme of “Journeys,” Spectra Arts teacher Teal Derrer partnered with Bagniefski to create “Journey Dogs,” an arts curriculum where students designed and created a unique stuffed dog.  Derrer believes that “everybody has creativity inside them,” and that her role is to help them find it. 

Once the dogs were completed, Bagniefski then guided the students to use their persuasive writing skills to request, from friends and relatives around the world, adventures and a safe return home for their dogs.  Dogs traveled to Antarctica and Japan, and another became a companion for an elderly relative. 

In 2011 the President’s Committee on the Arts and Humanities issued a report that stated, “Children who practiced a specific art form developed improved attention skills and improved general intelligence.”  For Bagniefski, this Spectra Art-inspired project was so engaging to students, she created additional lessons in geography and creative writing.  The dogs inspired comic strips and a start-up business called Journey Dog Accessories (JDA), which sold booties, hats, belts and suitcases for the dogs.  Spectra Arts teacher Teal said that the project “took on a life of its own,” and a “self-generated” economy and barter system, including currency, developed to facilitate merchandise trading.   Bagniefski acknowledges that this learning extension wouldn’t have happened without the partnership of PiE’s Spectra Art program.
