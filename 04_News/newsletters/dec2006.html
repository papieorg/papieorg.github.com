    • High School Guidance: Paly and Gunn's Different Paths to Student Success

    Kaye Paugh is one of 16 teacher advisors at Palo Alto High School. Besides teaching English courses, Paugh leads grade level advisory classes for students in grades 10 through 12. She meets every week with one grade level class -- 27-28 students in each. She also coordinates with the counselors in Paly's College and Career Center (CCC) and other school staff to ensure that all 80 of her advisees are on track for meeting their high school graduation requirements and preparing for what lies ahead.

    In January Myesha Compton, currently an attendance secretary at Gunn High School, will become Gunn's sixth full time guidance counselor in a system that is modeled more along traditional lines. Compton will be responsible for roughly 300 students, freshmen through seniors. (She is completing graduate studies in counseling.)

    In some ways, the two schools' guidance models have much in common. Both employ a network of counselors and teachers who often collaborate to address the curricular, academic, and mental health needs of their students. Both have CCCs which house information about colleges, college fairs, SAT/ACT/AP test preparation materials, vocational training, work-study programs, scholarships, and financial aid. But Paly's 15-year-old teacher advisory program and Gunn's guidance model reflect the schools' unique approaches, said Kimberley Cowell, Assistant Principal for Guidance Services at Gunn.



    Gunn's guidance counselors, experts in their fields, are well-versed in the college application process, talk to college administrators and reps regularly and "are very much up-to-date on what colleges expect," said Cowell. They schedule individual conferences with every student at least once a year -- and students and parents often request additional meetings. Counselors also write personal letters of recommendation for all their students.

    This year Gunn guidance is putting a focus on ethics and student stress, which are inextricably related, Cowell said. "When you're desperate, what are you willing to do? We're working to help the students develop better coping mechanisms than cheating. We want our students to be ethical individuals when they graduate from Gunn," she said, adding that Gunn guidance counselors work with teachers, parents, outside counselors, and health professionals. Gunn has also begun holding intensive three-day orientations for freshmen to get them started on the right path.

    At Paly, freshmen are initially assigned teacher advisors, but at the end of that year, they pick new ones who remain their advisors for the rest of their time at Paly. Aside from seeing their counselors in Advisory, students can also meet with them individually. "Students drop by all the time to ask questions, get advice, or share something with us," said Paugh, adding that TAs also may know their advisees from being -- or from having been -- one of their teachers at Paly.

    Paly students also have access to college advisors in the school's CCC. Sandra Cernobori, a certificated college advisor, was hired in April of last year, and Briana Peikert, a part-time college advisor, began in August. All students meet with one of them at least once and most come in for appointments twice or more, according to Cernobori. She said she appreciates that she has a somewhat unique position, being a certificated advisor. "It's unusual for a public school in California to have a certificated college advisor," she said. "The job description is not common elsewhere. It's not typically built into school district funding," she said.

    Funding from Palo Alto Partners in Education (PiE) supports the guidance program at Paly and is just one of the great ways that principals can use PiE dollars to help their schools. PiE is the only non-profit foundation dedicated to supporting all schools in the Palo Alto Unified School District -- and the only non- district source for school staff funding.
