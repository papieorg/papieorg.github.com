IN OUR MIDDLE SCHOOLS

New Leadership, New Learning

Although the three PAUSD middle school principals are all relatively new to their current positions, they have already forged a strong team, sharing ideas to meet ongoing challenges, evaluating current programs, and continuing to create excellence in middle school learning environments. Superintendent Kevin Skelly is confident this new leadership has been good for the district as a whole, “Change is good for an organization. For our Principals, it’s been a good opportunity for them to grow, even if they’re only moving from one school to another. Change has brought an energy and appreciation for what we’re building here in terms of culture.”

In 2011, the Center for the Future of Teaching and Learning at WestEd reported that the state's budget cutbacks present significant new challenges to both classroom teachers and Principals, who are struggling to find the time, resources and capacity they need to meet the dual challenges of effective school management and ensuring quality instruction for California's students.

JLS Principal Sharon Ofek, who spent several years as Assistant Principal at Jordan, says, “We do a lot of work around teacher support, because our teachers are the ones creating the classroom environments in which students of all abilities can thrive.” She adds, “Our teachers are the point of entry. They are providing access for multiple levels of learners, a challenge that is ongoing, and at the core of our school mission: to shape a culture that supports all of its members.” She and the other middle school principals appreciate essential PiE donations that support Teacher Mentors like Karin Fossell and James Sperry, who ensure that teachers use technology effectively, maximizing teaching and learning time.

PiE funds provide community support that helps teachers achieve their goals, according to Katherine Baker, Terman Principal. She believes, “The ideas that inform the direction we take as a school bubble up from the teachers, who are, themselves, informed by the children and how they are learning.”

Ofek agrees. “Our teachers love their work. They share any new knowledge from the larger education community enthusiastically with their colleagues, and we are all enriched.” Superintendent Kevin Skelly, who encouraged many of the district’s new leaders to attend an education conferences at Harvard last summer, believes that nurturing school leadership through continued professional learning opportunities positively affects school climate and classroom improvement, “That’s one of the ways we’re trying to build culture in our district. I want our staff to have a shared learning experience in a vibrant, learning environment.”

PiE funds underwrite many of the innovations that come from these experiences. “PiE funding is about making hard choices easier,” declares Greg Barnes, Jordan Middle School’s new principal. “By giving me the flexibility to fund new electives, or special projects that will enhance learning, or to provide support in a classroom that will ultimately facilitate more efficient teaching and learning…this is how I see the tremendous impact PiE has on our ability to implement great ideas quickly and strategically.”

Barnes spends a lot of his time thinking about school culture. “The student-run programs and events that further a sense of shared culture are invaluable to a cohesive school community,” Barnes says. Counselors at all three middle schools, together with essential PiE dollars, support many of these community-building events, including those that guide students through the transition from elementary to middle school. Because of PiE, middle school counselors now also travel with their cohorts through all three years of middle school, further enabling that student-to-adult ratio so important for building a strong connection to school.
